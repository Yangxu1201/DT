# Design: Manager-Subordinate Reporting Agent

## Problem Statement

This product is an internal work operating system for subordinates who struggle to respond upward in a way their manager can reliably feel.

The core user is someone like Zhang San:
- Hardware product manager
- Also acting as project manager, procurement manager, certification engineer, brand marketing, Reddit community operator, Amazon operator, and AI learner
- Soft personality, agreeable, tends to carry things alone
- Learned from past jobs to report only after results exist

That pattern breaks under a manager who wants constant visibility. In practice, the subordinate often reads a request, replies politely or mentally says "received," then gets pulled into other work and forgets. The manager experiences this as lack of initiative, weak ownership, and poor prioritization.

This means the real problem is not "how to word a better reply." The real problem is turning a manager's vague request into:
- a clear mainline task
- an execution plan
- an explicit decision boundary
- a follow-up loop that does not drop
- a response that matches the manager's communication style

## Demand Evidence

Strongest evidence from this session:
- Zhang San's manager asks for project risk updates at 10 PM.
- Zhang San often does not reply in a useful way, or replies and then forgets.
- If there is no agent, the next day he is likely to be seen as not proactive, which affects trust and performance.
- The user described this as an existing real workflow, not a hypothetical need.

Additional evidence:
- The manager asks across many domains every day.
- The subordinate's current workaround is local prioritization in his own head, which the manager cannot see.
- Important non-urgent tasks, such as AI learning, get acknowledged and then lost once urgent noise arrives.

This is real demand because the pain is not abstract. It changes trust, perceived reliability, and performance evaluation.

## Status Quo

Current workflow:
1. The manager sends fragmented instructions across many domains.
2. Zhang San informally decides what feels most urgent.
3. If information is incomplete, or there is no immediate time, he mentally "receives" the task first.
4. He often does not convert that into a tracked next step or upward feedback.
5. After one to two days, the item may be forgotten.

Current cost:
- The manager cannot feel the true workload complexity.
- The manager sees weak responsiveness instead of hidden effort.
- Tasks drift away from the manager's intended point.
- The subordinate carries decisions he should not own.
- Long-term strategic tasks lose to short-term noise.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

### Target User

Primary user:
- A subordinate who is capable and hardworking but weak at upward management
- Soft personality
- Tends to over-carry
- Not naturally good at translating work-in-progress into manager-readable updates
- Wants to become a stronger, more opinionated, AI-native product or business leader

Primary manager type:
- Wants response to everything
- Works remotely and worries subordinates are not working
- Wants high information density
- Expects proactive outreach
- Likes setting rules
- Has low patience
- Does not naturally protect subordinate time

### Narrowest Wedge

Even though the long-term vision is a full reporting system, the smallest credible wedge is:

When a manager sends one message, the system produces:
- a structured action card
- a decision-boundary judgment
- an immediate manager-facing response
- a follow-up record so the item does not disappear

This wedge is the seed for the larger system.

## Constraints

- The system must support a subordinate wearing many hats at once.
- It must distinguish between tasks the subordinate can decide, tasks he can recommend on, and tasks that require manager approval.
- It must not become a "polite reply generator" that hides lack of execution.
- It must preserve the manager's sense of visibility without forcing the subordinate into constant manual status-writing.
- It must support long-running, non-urgent but strategic work, not only urgent pings.

## Premises

1. The first job of the product is to prevent missed, wrong, or over-owned responses to manager instructions.
2. The real product is a work-structuring system, not a language-polishing tool.
3. A subordinate like Zhang San does not mainly fail because of low effort. He fails because vague requests are not converted into explicit mainline work.
4. Decision ownership must be explicit, or the subordinate will keep carrying things he should escalate.
5. The system must work at two layers:
   - a general work-structuring layer
   - a manager-preference adaptation layer
6. If the system gets style slightly wrong, recovery is possible. If it drops a task, trust collapses faster.
7. Reports like daily, weekly, and monthly updates should be outputs of tracked work, not standalone writing exercises.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Reply-First Assistant

Summary:
Take each manager message and generate a stronger immediate reply, with light reasoning behind it.

Effort:
S

Risk:
Medium

Pros:
- Fastest to demo
- Immediate manager-visible effect
- Helps the subordinate break the silence habit

Cons:
- Easily degenerates into a wording tool
- Weak protection against dropped work
- Mainline task judgment stays shallow

Reuses:
- The nighttime manager-message scenario

### Approach B: Action-Card Core

Summary:
Convert each manager message into a structured action card first, then generate the reply from that card.

Effort:
M

Risk:
Low

Pros:
- Strong product spine
- Supports tracking and later reports naturally
- Less likely to become fake productivity theater

Cons:
- Less emotionally impressive than a very polished reply demo
- Requires stronger field design up front
- Does not fully express the manager-style layer

Reuses:
- The six core fields identified in discussion

### Approach C: Dual-Layer Work Operating System

Summary:
Build a two-layer system:
- Bottom layer: task decomposition, boundary judgment, follow-up tracking, report generation
- Top layer: manager personality and communication adaptation

Effort:
L

Risk:
High

Pros:
- Matches the user's actual product ambition
- Handles both execution truth and relationship fit
- Can evolve into a reusable internal reporting system across managers

Cons:
- Easy to overscope
- Requires clear separation between core logic and manager-style overlay
- First version can become bloated if not tightly staged

Reuses:
- The manager type described in the session
- The subordinate profile and real failure pattern already observed

## Recommended Approach

Choose Approach C, but stage it hard.

The product should be designed as a dual-layer system because the user correctly identified two first-order needs:
- the system must understand how this manager wants to be informed
- the system must never let work disappear after a polite acknowledgment

But the implementation sequence inside Approach C should still be disciplined:

### Layer 1: Work Core

Every incoming manager instruction becomes a structured work object with at least:
1. My thinking
2. My plan
3. Timeline
4. Expected output
5. Needed resources
6. Decision ownership

Recommended additional operational fields:
7. Mainline priority
8. Current status
9. Next manager-facing update due at
10. Risk or blocker

### Layer 2: Manager Adaptation

Based on prior conversations and explicit profile signals, the system decides:
- response density
- how fast to acknowledge
- whether to lead with conclusion or detail
- how often to proactively update
- when to surface risk early

### First Demo

The best first demo is:

Manager sends one message ->
system outputs:
- mainline task
- next step
- timeline
- whether manager approval is needed
- immediate response draft
- follow-up reminder/record

This proves both layers in one flow without pretending the entire operating system is already complete.

## Open Questions

1. How much of the manager profile should be inferred from chat history versus explicitly configured?
2. How should the system distinguish "important but not urgent strategic work" from noisy short-term pings?
3. Should follow-up be fully automated, or should the subordinate explicitly confirm/own each scheduled next step?
4. What level of decision recommendation is safe in non-product domains like supplier choice, certification, and marketing?
5. How should the product behave when multiple manager requests conflict in timeline or priority?

## Success Criteria

User-visible success:
- The subordinate replies faster without sounding empty
- The subordinate misses fewer manager requests
- The subordinate escalates decision-boundary issues more clearly
- The manager feels more visibility and less need to chase

Behavioral success:
- "Received and forgot" decreases sharply
- More messages contain explicit next steps and timing
- More tasks are tracked to closure
- Daily, weekly, and monthly reports reflect actual tracked work, not reconstructed memory

Manager outcome success:
- The manager describes the subordinate as more proactive
- The manager feels the subordinate is "thinking like an owner"
- The manager needs fewer manual follow-ups

## Distribution Plan

Initial distribution:
- Internal use by one subordinate-manager pair first
- Feed prior chat history and role context manually
- Use as a supervised workflow assistant before deeper automation

Operational rollout:
- Pilot with one subordinate profile and one manager profile
- Expand to more managers only after the work core is stable

Publishing:
- No external distribution required initially
- If the product later expands internally, the manager adaptation layer should be configurable instead of hard-coded

## Dependencies

- Historical chat logs between manager and subordinate
- A stable schema for structured work objects
- A manager-profile representation
- A rule set for decision ownership across domains
- A follow-up mechanism that is trusted and visible

## The Assignment

Before building anything, collect 20 real manager messages from this one manager and label each one manually with:
- intended task
- desired output
- deadline or implied urgency
- whether the subordinate can decide or only recommend
- what a good immediate reply would look like
- what follow-up should have happened after that

If this labeling exercise is painful or ambiguous, that is good news. It means the product surface is real. Start there, not in code.
